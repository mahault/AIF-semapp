\begin{abstract}
We argue that the SemApp semiotic data fabric, an operational, petabyte-scale data architecture deployed within the U.S.\ Intelligence Community, constitutes a cognitive substrate in the formal sense defined by the Free Energy Principle.
We establish a structural isomorphism between C.~S.~Peirce's triadic semiotics and variational inference, showing that SemApp's Sign Representation Framework implements semiosis as free energy minimization.
We show that SemApp's three-layer data architecture (unstructured data, structured Signs, domain models) induces nested Markov blankets, with each layer satisfying the conditional independence requirements of the blanket partition under an explicit graphical model of the architecture.
We formalize Process Waves as active inference agents whose expected free energy decomposes into deontic, epistemic, and pragmatic components mapped to concrete SemApp operations.
We derive the fabric-level variational free energy $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ from the fabric's generative model under a mean-field factorization, and argue that cumulative Wave processing tends to decrease this quantity under consistent domain models, a claim validated empirically by simulation.
A computational demonstration using discrete active inference following the \texttt{pymdp} generative model specification \citep{heins2022pymdp} validates the formal mapping through four experiments of increasing scope: (1)~Artifact Standard Detection, where a single Wave infers file formats from noisy cues, outperforming rule-based and maximum-likelihood baselines on ambiguous artifacts; (2)~ambiguity resolution, where progressive context accumulation enables a Wave to resolve polysemous Signs; (3)~multi-Wave entity resolution, where domain-specialized Waves cooperating through belief propagation via stigmergic links outperform isolated, generalist, and homogeneous configurations; and (4)~nested free energy minimization across an integrated fabric of 100~elements, demonstrating monotonic decrease of fabric-level uncertainty with discrete jumps at the element level.
These results establish that a semiotic data fabric organized into nested Markov blankets, with decentralized active inference agents operating over meaningful Signs, satisfies the formal criteria for cognition under the Free Energy Principle---providing both explanatory power for why SemApp works at scale and predictive power for next-generation data architecture design.

\vspace{6pt}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Active inference, Free Energy Principle, Markov blankets, semiotics, data fabric, Peircean signs, distributed cognition, ultra-large-scale systems, process waves, variational inference
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The Intelligence Problem in Ultra-Large-Scale Data Environments}
\label{sec:uls}

The national defense enterprise of the United States has a data problem. Despite spending billions annually on data collection, storage, processing, and management, the Intelligence Community (IC) and Department of Defense (DoD) struggle to capitalize on those investments \citep{sei2006uls, yoakumstover2025semapp}. The consequences to national security are serious: intelligence and military operations are impeded as basic command challenges go unmet.

The root cause, as identified by the Software Engineering Institute's landmark study \citep{sei2006uls}, is that the defense data ecosystem constitutes an \emph{Ultra-Large-Scale} (ULS) system, characterized by decentralization, inherently conflicting requirements, heterogeneous elements, normal failures, continuous evolution, and immense scale along many dimensions. ULS systems are to ordinary systems as cities are to buildings: qualitatively different in kind, not merely in degree.

Traditional approaches to data unification, \emph{harmonization} (merging data models into a canonical form) and \emph{federation} (translating through adapters to a canonical model), both fail at ULS scale because they attempt to impose a single semantic perspective on inherently diverse data. There is no one right way to represent all knowledge that will suffice for every purpose. To impose one destroys the essential complexity that gives intelligence its richness \citep{yoakumstover2025semapp}.

The conundrum is that unifying data means having one interface to all data, which implies a single data model, entailing a single fixed perspective. But there is no one right way to represent all knowledge, and to impose one thwarts the ULS nature of the problem.

This paper proposes that this conundrum dissolves when viewed through the lens of active inference and the Free Energy Principle (FEP). The data problem is a \emph{cognitive} challenge. At ULS scale, a data system must \emph{make sense} of its contents which means discovering patterns, resolving ambiguities, maintaining coherent representations while accommodating diversity. These are precisely the functions that the FEP attributes to cognitive systems.

We argue that the SemApp data fabric, developed by Mission Focus and operational on classified IC networks processing nearly 70 petabytes of diverse data \citep{yoakumstover2025semapp}, resolves this conundrum because, whether by design or convergent evolution, it has the architecture of a cognitive system. Specifically, it implements decentralized active inference through nested Markov blankets over a semiotic substrate.

\subsection{Active Inference as the Physics of Intelligence}
\label{sec:aif_intro}

The FEP is a first-principles account of why self-organizing systems persist and how they maintain their identity in a changing world \citep{friston2019particular, albarracin2025resilience}. At its core, the FEP states that any system that maintains a boundary between itself and its environment, formally, a \emph{Markov blanket}, must minimize an information-theoretic quantity called \emph{variational free energy}. This minimization is equivalent to performing approximate Bayesian inference. The system implicitly maintains a model of its environment and acts to confirm its predictions.

Active inference extends this principle to agents that can act on their environment \citep{parr2022active}. Under active inference, perception is inference about the causes of sensory data, learning is inference about model parameters, and action is inference about the best policy, the course of action that will minimize expected future surprise. The framework naturally decomposes action selection into epistemic (information-seeking) and pragmatic (goal-directed) components, providing a unified account of curiosity, exploration, and exploitation.

Crucially, the FEP is \emph{scale-free}. The same principles apply at every level of organization, from molecular interactions to neural circuits to social groups \citep{friston2019particular, kirchhoff2018markov, ramstead2023inner}. When a system contains subsystems that themselves have Markov blankets, these blankets \emph{nest}, creating a hierarchical architecture in which each level performs its own active inference while serving as a component of the level above. This multi-scale self-organization is what distinguishes genuinely cognitive systems from merely physical ones \citep{kirchhoff2018markov}.

Recent work has proposed that these principles can guide the design of ecosystems of natural and artificial intelligence \citep{friston2022ecosystems}. Rather than building monolithic AI systems, one can engineer distributed networks of intelligent agents that coordinate through shared generative models, forming emergent collective intelligence at superordinate scales. The substrate for this coordination, the shared medium through which agents exchange beliefs and observations, is what we identify with the semiotic data fabric.

\subsection{The Peircean Bridge: Semiotics Meets Statistical Physics}
\label{sec:peirce_intro}

The missing link between data architecture and cognitive science is \emph{semiotics}, the study of signs and meaning-making. C.~S.~Peirce's triadic semiotics describes meaning as an inferential process: a \emph{sign} (representamen) stands for an \emph{object} (referent) to produce an \emph{interpretant} (meaning) in the mind of an interpreter \citep{peirce1931collected}. This is not a static relation but a \emph{process}, \emph{semiosis}, in which each interpretant becomes a new sign, generating unlimited chains of meaning.

Recent work in active inference has established a formal isomorphism between Peircean semiosis and variational inference \citep{constant2019regimes, ramstead2016cultural, albarracin2022epistemic}. The sign maps to an observation, the object maps to a hidden state, and the interpretant maps to the updated posterior belief. Semiosis, the generation of meaning from signs, \emph{is} variational inference, described in a different vocabulary.

This isomorphism is not merely an analogy. As \citet{albarracin2025nonmarkovian} argue, meaning and context are inherently non-Markovian. The interpretation of a sign depends on the full history of prior interpretations. The FEP accommodates this through temporal depth in generative models and through the non-Markovian dynamics that arise when systems maintain identity over time.

SemApp's fundamental data primitive, the \emph{Sign}, which binds a \emph{Signifier} (data value) with a \emph{Concept} (meaning), is a computational implementation of Peirce's triadic sign. This paper formalizes this connection and shows that SemApp's entire architecture can be understood as a system performing active inference over semiotic content.

\subsection{Contributions and Paper Overview}
\label{sec:contributions}

This paper develops a unified account of semiotic processes and variational inference, making several key contributions. First, we establish a formal correspondence between Peircean semiosis and variational inference (Section~\ref{sec:signs_vfe}), demonstrating that the creation of Signs in SemApp can be understood as minimizing a form of semiotic free energy. Building on this, we show that SemApp's three-layer architecture naturally induces a hierarchy of nested Markov blankets (Section~\ref{sec:nested}), such that conditional independence holds between Artifacts (external states), Signs (blanket states), and domain models (internal states) under an explicit graphical model of the architecture.

We then introduce a formal account of Process Waves as active inference agents, and demonstrate how the DEEP value decomposition \citep{constant2019regimes} maps directly onto concrete operations within SemApp. Extending this framework, we derive the fabric-level variational free energy $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ from the fabric's generative model under a mean-field factorization (Section~\ref{sec:fmaterial}), and argue that it tends to decrease under consistent Wave processing, a property validated by simulation. Finally, we provide a computational demonstration using discrete active inference following the generative model specification of \texttt{pymdp} \citep{heins2022pymdp}, validating the formal correspondence through four experiments on a simulated semiotic fabric that progress from single-Wave Artifact classification to multi-scale free energy minimization across an integrated fabric.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews the theoretical foundations, including the Free Energy Principle, Peircean semiotics, and the SemApp architecture. Section~\ref{sec:formal} develops the formal mapping and constitutes the mathematical core of the paper. Section~\ref{sec:fmaterial} derives the fabric-level free energy, while Section~\ref{sec:simulation} presents the computational experiments. Section~\ref{sec:implications} discusses the broader implications for active inference and defense data architectures. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} outlines limitations and future directions, and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Background and Theoretical Foundations}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{The Free Energy Principle and Active Inference}
\label{sec:fep}

\subsubsection{Variational Free Energy and the Generative Model}
\label{sec:vfe}

The Free Energy Principle begins with a simple observation. Things that exist must, by definition, maintain their existence, they must resist the tendency toward disorder mandated by the second law of thermodynamics. Any system that persists in a characteristic set of states can be described as if it possesses a boundary, a \emph{Markov blanket}, that separates its internal states from external states, and as if its internal dynamics implement a \emph{generative model} of how its sensory observations are caused by external states \citep{friston2019particular}.

Variational free energy is a tractable objective for approximate Bayesian inference. In our formulation, $\bo$ denotes observations (sensory states), $\bs$ hidden states of the environment, $\bq(\bs)$ an approximate posterior (the system's beliefs), and $\bp(\bo, \bs)$ a generative model describing how observations arise from hidden states. The variational free energy is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:vfe}
\mathcal{F} = \E_{\bq(\bs)}\!\left[\ln \bq(\bs) - \ln \bp(\bo, \bs)\right]
= \KL\!\left[\bq(\bs) \,\|\, \bp(\bs|\bo)\right] - \ln \bp(\bo)
\end{equation}

Since the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence is non-negative, $\mathcal{F}$ upper bounds surprise (negative log evidence), i.e., $\mathcal{F} \geq -\ln \bp(\bo)$. Minimizing $\mathcal{F}$ with respect to $\bq$ therefore serves two complementary roles. It drives the approximate posterior $\bq(\bs)$ toward the true posterior $\bp(\bs|\bo)$ (perception), while simultaneously maximizing a lower bound on model evidence $\ln \bp(\bo)$ (self-evidencing).

The generative model typically factorizes as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gm}
\bp(\bo, \bs) = \bp(\bo|\bs)\,\bp(\bs)
\end{equation}
where $\bp(\bo|\bs)$ is the likelihood mapping hidden states to observations, and $\bp(\bs)$ encodes prior beliefs over hidden states.

In the discrete-state active inference framework \citep{parr2022active}, the generative model is typically parameterized by four sets of matrices. The $\mathbf{A}$ matrix encodes the likelihood mapping $\bp(\bo|\bs)$, specifying how hidden states generate observations. The $\mathbf{B}$ matrix captures transition dynamics $\bp(\bs_{t+1}|\bs_t, \ba_t)$, describing how hidden states evolve over time under the influence of actions. Preferences over outcomes are encoded by the $\mathbf{C}$ matrix, which corresponds to log-preferences $\ln \bp(\bo)$ and determines which observations are expected or desired. Finally, the $\mathbf{D}$ matrix specifies prior beliefs over initial states $\bp(\bs_0)$.

These matrices fully define the agent’s generative model. In what follows, we will map each of these components onto corresponding structures within the SemApp architecture.

\subsubsection{Expected Free Energy and the DEEP Decomposition}
\label{sec:efe}

Active inference extends perception to action. An agent selects policies (sequences of actions) $\bpi$ that minimize \emph{expected free energy}, the free energy the agent expects to encounter in the future if it follows a given policy.

Expected free energy gives us the objective function for policy selection under active inference. For a given policy $\bpi$ at a future time $\tau$, it is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:efe}
G(\bpi, \tau) = \E_{\tilde{\bq}}\!\left[\ln \bq(\bs_\tau | \bpi) - \ln \bp(\bo_\tau, \bs_\tau)\right]
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\bq} = \bq(\bo_\tau, \bs_\tau | \bpi)$ denotes the predicted joint distribution over future observations and hidden states under policy $\bpi$. Intuitively, this quantity evaluates the expected divergence between predicted beliefs and the generative model over future trajectories.

This objective can be decomposed into distinct functional contributions:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:deep}
G(\bpi, \tau) = \underbrace{-\E_{\tilde{\bq}}\!\left[\ln \bp(\bo_\tau)\right]}_{\text{Pragmatic value}} + \underbrace{-\E_{\tilde{\bq}}\!\left[\mathcal{H}\!\left[\bp(\bo_\tau|\bs_\tau)\right]\right]}_{\text{Epistemic value (ambiguity)}}
\end{equation}
The first term captures pragmatic (or extrinsic) value, favouring outcomes that align with prior preferences, while the second term reflects epistemic value, encouraging policies that reduce ambiguity by sampling informative observations.

Following \citet{constant2019regimes}, this decomposition can be further refined into DEEP components. These distinguish between deontic value, arising from conformity to environmental norms and cues; epistemic value, associated with information gain about hidden states; extrinsic or pragmatic value, linked to achieving preferred outcomes; and preferential value, reflecting prior expectations over observations.

Policy selection then proceeds by minimizing expected free energy over future time:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:policy}
\bpi^* = \arg\min_{\bpi} \sum_{\tau} G(\bpi, \tau)
\end{equation}
so that agents choose actions that balance goal-directed behaviour with uncertainty reduction.

\subsubsection{Nested Markov Blankets and Multi-Scale Self-Organization}
\label{sec:nested_blankets}

A Markov blanket formalizes the boundary of a ``thing'', the minimal set of states that renders a system's internal states conditionally independent of its external environment.

A system admits a Markov blanket partition when its state space $\mathcal{X}$ can be decomposed into four classes of states:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:blanket}
\mathcal{X} = \bmu \cup \bs \cup \ba \cup \boldeta
\end{equation}
where $\bmu$ denote internal states, $\bs$ sensory states, $\ba$ active states, and $\boldeta$ external states. This partition is defined by a conditional independence structure such that:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ci}
\bp(\bmu, \boldeta \,|\, \bs, \ba) = \bp(\bmu \,|\, \bs, \ba) \cdot \bp(\boldeta \,|\, \bs, \ba)
\end{equation}

In this formulation, the sensory and active states together form the Markov blanket, $\mathcal{B} = \bs \cup \ba$, which mediates all statistical dependencies between internal and external states. External states can influence internal states only via sensory states, while internal states can influence external states only through active states. This separation induces a structured interface between a system and its environment, enabling the formulation of perception and action as coupled inferential processes.

The crucial insight of \citet{friston2019particular} is that Markov blankets \emph{nest}. If a system has a Markov blanket, and its internal states themselves admit Markov blanket partitions, then one obtains a recursive, hierarchical structure. At each level, the same active inference dynamics operate, perception, action, and free energy minimization, while the whole system self-organizes into a multi-scale cognitive architecture \citep{kirchhoff2018markov, ramstead2023inner}.

This nesting is not optional or decorative. As \citet{kirchhoff2018markov} argue in ``The Markov Blankets of Life,'' the recursive nesting of blankets is precisely what distinguishes genuinely cognitive (living) systems from merely physical ones. The inner screen hypothesis of \citet{ramstead2023inner} further shows that the deepest level of this nested hierarchy, the irreducible internal Markov blanket, constitutes the computational substrate for the most integrated forms of information processing, including consciousness.

For our purposes, the key implication is architectural. \emph{Any system organized into nested Markov blankets, with active inference dynamics at each level, constitutes a cognitive system in the formal sense defined by the FEP}. This is implementation-independent: the FEP makes no requirement on the physical substrate, only on the informational structure \citep{fields2025inner}.


\subsection{Peircean Semiotics as Inferential Process}
\label{sec:peirce}

\subsubsection{The Sign Triad and Unlimited Semiosis}

C.~S.~Peirce's semiotics describes meaning as a \emph{process}, \emph{semiosis}, in which a \emph{sign} (representamen) stands for an \emph{object} (referent) to produce an \emph{interpretant} (meaning) in the mind of an interpreter \citep{peirce1931collected}. The interpretant is itself a sign, capable of generating further interpretants in an open-ended chain that Peirce termed \emph{unlimited semiosis}.

This process-oriented view of meaning aligns naturally with the dynamical perspective of active inference. Rather than asking ``What does this datum mean?'' (a static question), semiosis asks ``What meaning does this datum generate in the context of this interpreter's model?'' (a dynamical question). The answer depends on the interpreter's generative model, its priors, likelihood mappings, and preferences, making meaning inherently perspectival and context-dependent.

\subsubsection{Semiosis as Abductive Inference}

Peirce himself characterized semiosis as a form of \emph{abduction}, inference to the best explanation. Given an observation (sign), the interpreter abduces the most plausible hidden cause (object) and forms a belief (interpretant) accordingly. This is precisely what variational inference does: given an observation $\bo$, it finds the distribution $\bq(\bs)$ that best explains $\bo$ under the generative model $\bp(\bo, \bs)$.


There exists a structural isomorphism between Peircean semiosis and variational inference under the FEP:

\vspace{4pt}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Peirce} & \textbf{Active Inference} & \textbf{Formal Object} \\
\midrule
Sign (Representamen) & Observation & $\bo$ \\
Object (Referent) & Hidden state & $\bs$ \\
Interpretant (Meaning) & Posterior belief & $\bq(\bs|\bo)$ \\
Semiosis (sign process) & Variational inference & $\bq^* = \arg\min_{\bq} \mathcal{F}$ \\
Unlimited semiosis & Recursive belief updating & $\bq_{t+1} \leftarrow \bq_t$ \\
Ground (context) & Generative model & $\bp(\bo, \bs)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{4pt}
\noindent This is not merely an analogy. The mathematics are identical. In both frameworks, an observation constrains a posterior belief through a model, and the resulting belief becomes the input for subsequent inference. The isomorphism holds at the level of the variational calculus.


\subsubsection{From Individual Semiosis to Shared Meaning}

Human cognition is fundamentally distributed. We think \emph{through other minds} by maintaining shared generative models \citep{veissiere2020thinking}. Cultural scripts \citep{albarracin2021variational}, narratives \citep{bouizegarene2023narrative}, and social norms \citep{constant2019regimes} all function as shared priors that coordinate inference across individuals without centralized control.

\citet{guenincarlut2023embedded} introduce the concept of \emph{embedded normativity}. Norms and values are physically embedded in the material and social environment. The affordance landscape of a cultural niche, its tools, symbols, institutions, and data structures, carries normative content that shapes all inference performed within it. This is the theoretical foundation for treating a data fabric as a cognitive substrate. If norms can be embedded in material structures, and if those structures participate in active inference, then the fabric is not merely a tool that minds use, it is a constitutive part of the cognitive system.

\subsection{The SemApp Architecture}
\label{sec:semapp}

SemApp (Semiotic Apparatus) is a data fabric architecture developed by Mission Focus that resolves the data unification conundrum by decoupling data semantics from the data interface and data processing from data flow \citep{yoakumstover2025semapp}. It has been operational on classified Intelligence Community networks, processing nearly 70 petabytes of diverse data under the NGA GDAC program.

\subsubsection{The Sign Representation Framework (SRF)}

The atom of information in SemApp is the \textbf{Sign}, a computational element that binds a \emph{Signifier} (a binary blob representing any data value: number, string, symbol, image) with a \emph{Concept} (a meaning specified in some domain data model). A Sign may also carry a geometry (making it a GIS Feature), a temporal period (making it an Activity-Based Intelligence Event), and for quantities, a unit within a system of units.

Signs are connected through a small set of relational structures that define how meaning is composed and propagated within the fabric. Associations capture subject--predicate--object relations between Signs, where predicates are defined by domain models and encode the semantic structure of the system. Reification allows one or more Signs to be wrapped within another Sign, supporting aggregation, data cleansing, and the ability to reinterpret content across domain models. Context relations link descriptive Signs to a given subject Sign, enriching it with additional attributes, while mention relations provide provenance by connecting extracted Signs back to their source Artifacts.

Each element in the SRF carries a classification label, which governs data releasability, as well as a Perceiver, identifying the entity responsible for its creation—whether a user, application, Process Wave, or domain model. Signs are uniquely identified through hash-based IDs and are, with narrow exceptions, immutable, ensuring traceability and consistency across the fabric.

\subsubsection{The Three-Layer Architecture}

SemApp organizes data into a three-layer architecture that separates raw data, structured representations, and domain semantics. At the first layer, Artifacts provide a unified store for unstructured data of any kind. Rather than ingesting full data payloads, only lightweight reference records are persisted, enabling a ``no-byte ingest'' approach in which data can remain in place while still being accessible.

The second layer consists of Signs, which form a unified store for structured data. Signs are indexed across multiple dimensions—semantic, textual, geospatial, and temporal—making them directly discoverable through SemApp’s query services and enabling flexible retrieval across heterogeneous data sources.

At the third layer, Domain Models provide a unified representation of domain-specific schemas using an OWL-full-like formalism. This allows arbitrary domain models to be expressed without enforcing rigid standardization, while still supporting interoperability where needed.

Taken together, this architecture decouples the storage model from the domain model, allowing data from any source to be incorporated without constraining or altering its underlying semantics.

\subsubsection{The Process Wave Framework (PWF)}

The PWF replaces the traditional pipeline model with a distributed, wave-based approach to computation. Instead of pushing data through pre-configured pipelines, Waves execute concurrently across the entire data fabric, using a unified API to discover and operate on relevant data. In this paradigm, processing moves to the data rather than requiring data movement across systems.

A Process Wave is typically implemented as a containerized process that begins by identifying relevant input data based on specified characteristics through the unified API. It then performs its designated transformation or analysis, and asserts the resulting enrichment back onto the fabric in the form of new Signs and relations. As part of this process, each Wave leaves a fingerprint on the elements it has processed, enabling provenance tracking and deduplication.

Because all enrichments are written directly to the shared fabric, the outputs of any given Wave become immediately available to all other Waves, applications, and users. This creates a cumulative network effect. Each Wave builds on prior enrichments while contributing new structure and information for subsequent Waves to exploit.

\section{The Formal Mapping: SemApp as Active Inference}
\label{sec:formal}

\subsection{Signs as Variational Inference: The Semiosis--FEP Isomorphism}
\label{sec:signs_vfe}

We now formalize the claim that Sign creation in SemApp constitutes an act of variational inference. In this setting, binding a Signifier with a Concept corresponds to semiosis in the Peircean sense, and can be understood as minimizing a well-defined free energy functional.
$\sigma = (\text{Signifier}, \text{Concept})$ denote a Sign in the SRF. We interpret the Signifier $o_\sigma$ as an observed data value (a binary blob), and introduce a hidden variable $s$ corresponding to the real-world referent or state that the Sign is about. The Concept associated with the Sign, denoted $\bq_\sigma(s)$, is then a belief distribution over possible referents. The generative model is specified by a likelihood $\bp(o_\sigma | s)$, describing how referents generate observations, and a prior $\bp(s)$, encoding domain-level expectations (Layer 3).

Under this construction, the semiotic free energy of a Sign $\sigma$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:semiotic_fe}
\mathcal{F}_\sigma = \KL\!\left[\bq_\sigma(s) \,\|\, \bp(s|o_\sigma)\right] - \ln \bp(o_\sigma)
\end{equation}

Sign creation can then be understood as a process of semiosis that minimizes this quantity. Specifically, the system selects a Concept $\bq_\sigma$ that best explains the observed Signifier $o_\sigma$ under the generative model $\bp(o_\sigma, s) = \bp(o_\sigma|s)\bp(s)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:semiosis}
\bq_\sigma^* = \arg\min_{\bq_\sigma} \mathcal{F}_\sigma
\end{equation}

This optimum is attained when the Concept matches the posterior, $\bq_\sigma(s) = \bp(s|o_\sigma)$, at which point the semiotic free energy reduces to the negative log model evidence, $\mathcal{F}_\sigma = -\ln \bp(o_\sigma)$. In this sense, semiosis in SemApp can be viewed as a form of approximate Bayesian inference, grounding the creation of Signs in the minimization of free energy.

This construction follows directly from standard properties of variational free energy. In particular, by the non-negativity of the KL divergence, $\mathcal{F}_\sigma \geq -\ln \bp(o_\sigma)$, with equality if and only if $\bq_\sigma(s) = \bp(s|o_\sigma)$. This establishes that minimizing semiotic free energy corresponds to aligning the Concept with the posterior over referents given the observed Signifier.

In practice, Sign creation—whether performed by a human analyst, a Process Wave, or an ingest pipeline—requires selecting a Concept for a given Signifier. The quality of this assignment can be understood in terms of $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$: Concepts that accurately capture the underlying referent yield lower semiotic free energy, while mismatched or ambiguous assignments result in higher values.

Assuming that Sign creation processes are adapted to their domain—an assumption grounded in the role of domain models in Layer 3—the resulting Concept assignments can be viewed as approximating the minimizer of $\mathcal{F}_\sigma$. In this sense, semiosis operates as a practical instantiation of variational inference, even when the underlying optimization is implicit or distributed across heterogeneous processes.

Subject--Predicate--Object Associations between Signs encode conditional dependencies. The Association $(\sigma_i, \text{pred}, \sigma_j)$ encodes the belief $\bp(s_j | s_i, \text{pred})$. The network of Associations on the fabric is therefore a \emph{factor graph}, a graphical model in which variables (Signs) are connected by factors (Associations). Querying the fabric for related Signs is equivalent to performing \emph{belief propagation} on this factor graph: messages (beliefs about hidden states) flow along the edges, updating posterior beliefs at each node.
When a Sign $\sigma_{\text{high}}$ reifies (wraps) a set of lower-level Signs $\{\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_k\}$, it implements a \emph{hierarchical generative model}. The higher-level Sign represents a belief about the collection of lower-level Signs. In the language of active inference, reification introduces a new level in the generative model hierarchy:
\begin{equation}
\bp(\bo, \bs^{(1)}, \bs^{(2)}) = \bp(\bo | \bs^{(1)}) \cdot \bp(\bs^{(1)} | \bs^{(2)}) \cdot \bp(\bs^{(2)})
\end{equation}
where $\bs^{(1)}$ corresponds to the lower-level Signs and $\bs^{(2)}$ to the reifying Sign. This is variational message passing across levels of a deep generative model, the same mechanism that \citet{ramstead2023inner} identify as the basis for hierarchical processing in the brain.

When a Process Wave reads a Sign $\sigma_1$, processes it, and asserts a new enrichment Sign $\sigma_2$ (with a Mention link back to $\sigma_1$), this implements unlimited semiosis. The interpretant ($\sigma_2$) of the original sign ($\sigma_1$) becomes a new sign for subsequent interpreters. In active inference terms, the posterior $\bq_{\sigma_1}$ becomes a new observation for the next inference cycle, driving the recursive belief updating that \citet{albarracin2025nonmarkovian} argue is necessary for meaning in non-Markovian systems.


\subsection{The Three Layers as Nested Markov Blankets}
\label{sec:nested}

We now show that SemApp's three-layer architecture induces a Markov blanket structure, in which the Signs layer mediates all dependencies between raw data and domain models.

$\mathcal{X}_1$ denotes the set of Artifact states (Layer 1), $\mathcal{X}_2$ the set of Sign states (Layer 2), and $\mathcal{X}_3$ the set of domain model states (Layer 3). Under the SemApp architecture, these layers can be interpreted as forming a Markov blanket partition:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nested1}
\bp(\mathcal{X}_1, \mathcal{X}_3 \,|\, \mathcal{X}_2) = \bp(\mathcal{X}_1 \,|\, \mathcal{X}_2) \cdot \bp(\mathcal{X}_3 \,|\, \mathcal{X}_2)
\end{equation}

In this correspondence, Artifacts play the role of external states, $\boldeta = \mathcal{X}_1$, as they consist of raw, unstructured data. Domain models correspond to internal states, $\bmu = \mathcal{X}_3$, encoding the system’s generative assumptions about the world. The Signs layer forms the Markov blanket, $\mathcal{B} = \mathcal{X}_2$, mediating all interactions between these two levels.

This structure arises directly from the architectural constraints of SemApp. Artifacts do not directly reference domain models. They consist only of data and metadata, with no embedded semantic interpretation. Any linkage between an Artifact and a domain model must be established through Sign creation, in which a Process Wave extracts data and assigns Concepts drawn from a domain model. Conversely, domain models do not reference specific Artifacts; they define abstract Concepts and relations, and can only be instantiated through Signs that bind them to concrete data. As a result, all statistical dependencies between $\mathcal{X}_1$ and $\mathcal{X}_3$ are mediated by $\mathcal{X}_2$.

To make this claim precise, we specify a directed graphical model over the three layers. In the generative direction, domain models ($\mathcal{X}_3$) define the space of Concepts available for Sign creation; Signs ($\mathcal{X}_2$) bind these Concepts with data values drawn from Artifacts ($\mathcal{X}_1$). The joint distribution factorizes as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dag_factorization}
\bp(\mathcal{X}_1, \mathcal{X}_2, \mathcal{X}_3) = \bp(\mathcal{X}_1 \,|\, \mathcal{X}_2)\;\bp(\mathcal{X}_2 \,|\, \mathcal{X}_3)\;\bp(\mathcal{X}_3)
\end{equation}
corresponding to the directed acyclic graph $\mathcal{X}_3 \to \mathcal{X}_2 \to \mathcal{X}_1$. By d-separation, conditioning on the intermediate node $\mathcal{X}_2$ blocks the only path between $\mathcal{X}_1$ and $\mathcal{X}_3$, yielding the conditional independence in Equation~\ref{eq:nested1}. The absence of a direct $\mathcal{X}_1 \leftrightarrow \mathcal{X}_3$ edge is not an assumption imposed for mathematical convenience; it is an architectural invariant enforced by SemApp's API, which provides no mechanism for Artifacts and domain models to interact except through Signs. This graphical model is an idealization in the sense that side channels (e.g., file naming conventions that leak domain information into Artifact metadata) could introduce weak dependencies not captured by the DAG, but the architectural design deliberately minimizes such pathways.

Within the blanket layer, Signs can be further decomposed into sensory and active components. Signifiers and Mentions act as sensory states, carrying information from Artifacts into the system by encoding observed data values and their provenance. Associations, Context links, and Reification act as active states, imposing structure on the fabric and enabling downstream processing and discovery. In this sense, the Signs layer simultaneously receives information from the external world and acts back upon it through structured enrichment.

These properties show that the SemApp architecture implements a nested Markov blanket, with the Signs layer functioning as the interface through which external data and internal models are coupled.

This Markov blanket structure recurs at the level of individual Signs. Each Sign $\sigma$ can be understood as admitting its own local blanket partition. The internal states correspond to the Concept and Perceiver, which together encode the Sign’s semantic interpretation and provenance. Sensory states are given by the Signifier and Mentions, through which data enters from external sources and Artifacts. Active states are realized by Associations, Context, and Reification links, which impose structure on the surrounding fabric and propagate the Sign’s effects outward. Everything else in the system—other Signs, Artifacts, and domain models—constitutes the external states relative to the Sign.

In this way, each Sign functions as a localized interface between data and semantics, embedding a Markov blanket that both receives information from and acts upon the broader semiotic fabric.

This yields the nested structure that \citet{friston2019particular} describes, blankets within blankets, each level performing its own active inference. The Sign-level blanket is nested within the layer-level blanket, which is nested within the fabric-level blanket (which separates the organizational data estate from the external environment).

The nested blanket structure explains a key empirical observation about SemApp: new data sources and new Process Waves can be added without disrupting existing operations. This is because each new element creates its own Markov blanket, maintaining conditional independence from other elements at the same level. The architecture's resilience to change is not merely good engineering, it is a mathematical consequence of blanket integrity.



\subsection{Process Waves as Active Inference Agents}
\label{sec:waves}

Each Process Wave in SemApp can be formally characterized as an active inference agent operating on the semiotic fabric.
A Process Wave $w$ can be understood as instantiating its own generative model over the semiotic fabric, $\bp_w(\bo, \bs)$, parameterized in the standard active inference form. The likelihood mapping $\mathbf{A}_w$ defines $\bp_w(\bo|\bs)$ and corresponds to the Wave’s query template, specifying the patterns it expects to observe in the fabric. The transition model $\mathbf{B}_w$ encodes $\bp_w(\bs_{t+1}|\bs_t, \ba_t)$, capturing how the Wave expects the state of the fabric to evolve under its actions. Preferences are given by $\mathbf{C}_w = \ln \bp_w(\bo)$, describing which observations the Wave aims to realize through processing, while priors $\mathbf{D}_w = \bp_w(\bs_0)$ encode its initial beliefs about the state of the fabric.

Within this formulation, a Process Wave engages in a perception–action cycle over the fabric. During perception, the Wave queries the system via the unified API, receiving observations $\bo_t$ and updating its beliefs by minimizing variational free energy, $\bq_w(\bs_t) \leftarrow \arg\min_{\bq} \mathcal{F}_w(\bq, \bo_t)$. It then evaluates candidate actions in terms of their expected free energy:
\begin{equation}
G_w(\bpi, \tau) = \underbrace{-\E_{\tilde{\bq}_w}\!\left[\ln \bp_w(\bo_\tau)\right]}_{\text{Pragmatic}} + \underbrace{\E_{\tilde{\bq}_w}\!\left[\mathcal{H}\!\left[\bp_w(\bo_\tau|\bs_\tau)\right]\right]}_{\text{Epistemic}}
\end{equation}
balancing the drive to achieve preferred outcomes with the reduction of uncertainty. Action is then realized by asserting enrichments onto the fabric—creating new Signs, Associations, and Context links—while leaving a fingerprint that records provenance and enables coordination with other Waves.

In this sense, each Process Wave operates as an active inference agent embedded within the semiotic fabric, continuously updating its beliefs and acting to shape the informational structure of the system.

\paragraph{Current Waves vs.\ Active Inference Waves.} It is important to distinguish the formal characterization from the current implementation. Today's SemApp Process Waves are developer-configured, rule-based processors. For example, the Artifact Standard Detection (ASD) Wave uses a heuristic priority chain (magic bytes $\to$ file extension $\to$ parse attempt) to classify incoming files. Similarly, entity resolution and disambiguation Waves apply deterministic matching rules. The active inference formalization developed above shows how \emph{future} Waves could be designed with explicit generative models, Bayesian belief updating, and EFE-based action selection. The computational demonstration in Section~\ref{sec:simulation} directly compares the two approaches, showing that active inference Waves outperform their rule-based counterparts precisely on the ambiguous cases where rigid heuristics fail, while matching performance on clear-cut cases. This distinction is not a limitation of the formal mapping; rather, it reflects the fact that SemApp's architecture is already \emph{compatible} with active inference, and the framework provides a principled path to more adaptive Wave designs.

When a Wave leaves its fingerprint on a processed element, it creates a historical record that subsequent Waves can condition on. This transforms the fabric from a Markovian state space (where only the current state matters) into a non-Markovian one (where the processing history shapes future dynamics). As \citet{albarracin2025nonmarkovian} argue, this non-Markovianity is essential for meaning. The interpretation of a Sign depends not just on its current state but on its provenance, the chain of processes that created and enriched it.

In active inference terms, fingerprints extend the temporal depth of the generative model. A Wave with access to fingerprints can condition its beliefs on processing history, implementing the retention and protention that \citet{albarracin2025nonmarkovian} identify as necessary for phenomenological meaning-making.


\subsection{The Network Effect as Shared Markov Blanket Dynamics}
\label{sec:network}

A key property of SemApp’s Process Wave Framework is the emergence of a network effect. All enrichments are written directly to the shared fabric and immediately made available, and thus the actions of any given Wave alter the observations available to all others. In this sense, Waves do not operate independently, but become coupled through the semiotic environment they collectively construct.

This coupling can be understood in terms of a shared Markov blanket. When multiple Waves $w_1, \ldots, w_n$ operate over the same region of the fabric, they interact through a common subset of Signs, denoted $\mathcal{S}_{\text{shared}} \subset \mathcal{X}_2$. These shared Signs mediate dependencies between the Waves, such that the observations available to a given Wave $w_i$ depend not only on the state of the fabric, but also on the actions of other Waves:
\begin{equation}
\bp(o_{w_i} \mid \bs_{\text{shared}}, \ba_{w_j \neq i}) \neq \bp(o_{w_i} \mid \bs_{\text{shared}})
\end{equation}
As a result, each Wave’s perception is shaped by the interventions of others, and their generative models become implicitly coupled through the evolving structure of the fabric.

This mechanism gives rise to stigmergic coordination, in which agents coordinate indirectly by modifying a shared environment, rather than through explicit communication. In classical examples such as ant pheromone trails, environmental traces guide collective behaviour. Here, the shared semiotic fabric plays an analogous role. Waves leave structured traces in the form of Signs and relations, which in turn influence the inferences and actions of other Waves. From the perspective of active inference, this corresponds to overlapping Markov blankets, where agents that share environmental states effectively share portions of their generative models \citep{albarracin2022epistemic}.

\section{Fabric-Level Variational Inference and Embedded Normativity}
\label{sec:fmaterial}

\subsection{Embedded Normativity in Computational Infrastructure}

\citet{guenincarlut2023embedded} argue that normativity---the prescriptive structure that shapes what agents should do---is not confined to internal representations, but is embedded in the material environment. Tools, institutions, and data structures carry normative content that constrains inference and action for any agent interacting with them.

In SemApp, this normativity is embedded directly within the fabric through several interlocking mechanisms. Classification labels, attached to every SRF element, determine who can discover and access data; beyond simple access control, they function as deontic priors that constrain the hypothesis space available to any query. Domain models in Layer~3 further shape normativity by defining the Concepts available for Sign creation, effectively delimiting the space of possible meanings. Within a given domain model, an analyst's inferences are structured by its categories, until alternative framings are introduced through cross-domain links.

Normative structure is also carried by provenance. The Perceiver associated with each element records its origin, implicitly encoding trust and functioning as a precision weighting over beliefs derived from that element. In addition, policies such as label flooring---where the lowest classification is retained when conflicting classifications are present---introduce systematic biases toward openness, acting as a normative constraint on how information is propagated and shared.

From the perspective of extended cognition \citep{veissiere2020thinking}, these mechanisms imply that the fabric is not merely a passive repository or tool, but a constitutive component of the organizational cognitive system. Norms are not only represented by agents; they are enacted and stabilized within the environment itself, shaping how agents perceive, infer, and act.

\subsection{The Fabric-Level Generative Model}

The existence of a Markov blanket at the level of the fabric implies that the system can be described as maintaining a generative model over its observations and hidden states. We now make this model explicit and show how the fabric’s variational free energy decomposes into contributions from individual Signs.

At the fabric level, observations $\bo_{\text{fabric}}$ correspond to the accumulated Artifacts $\mathcal{O} = \{o_1, \ldots, o_N\}$, that is, the raw data ingested into Layer~1. The hidden states $\bs_{\text{fabric}}$ correspond to the underlying real-world entities, events, and relationships that this data reflects. The likelihood mapping $\bp(\bo_{\text{fabric}} \mid \bs_{\text{fabric}})$ captures how these latent states give rise to observable data, and is implemented implicitly through the Sign extraction process. The prior $\bp(\bs_{\text{fabric}})$ is given by the domain models in Layer~3, which encode expectations about what entities and relations exist.

The approximate posterior $\bq_{\text{fabric}}(\bs)$ is realized by the Sign store in Layer~2: the totality of Concept assignments across all Signs constitutes the system’s current belief state. Actions at the fabric level correspond to Wave processing, which asserts enrichments, associations, and reifications onto the fabric. Preferences are expressed through mission objectives, data quality constraints, and conformance to domain models.

Under this interpretation, the fabric’s variational free energy takes the standard form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:f_fabric_def}
\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}} = \KL\!\left[\bq_{\text{fabric}}(\bs) \,\|\, \bp(\bs \mid \bo_{\text{fabric}})\right] - \ln \bp(\bo_{\text{fabric}})
\end{equation}

\subsection{Mean-Field Decomposition}
\label{cor:f_fabric}

To make this expression tractable, we introduce three explicit assumptions. First, we adopt a \emph{mean-field posterior} in which the fabric's belief state factorizes across Signs as independent beliefs about distinct referents:
\begin{equation}
\bq_{\text{fabric}}(\bs) \approx \prod_{\sigma \in \mathcal{S}} \bq_\sigma(s_\sigma)
\end{equation}
Second, we assume a \emph{factorized likelihood}: each Artifact $o_\sigma$ depends only on the world state $s_\sigma$ that generated it, so that $\bp(\bo_{\text{fabric}} | \bs_{\text{fabric}}) = \prod_\sigma \bp(o_\sigma | s_\sigma)$. Third, we assume a \emph{factorized prior}: domain model expectations decompose as $\bp(\bs_{\text{fabric}}) = \prod_\sigma \bp_{\mathcal{M}}(s_\sigma)$.

Under these assumptions, the fabric-level VFE (Equation~\ref{eq:f_fabric_def}) expands as follows. Writing $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}} = \E_{\bq}[\ln \bq(\bs) - \ln \bp(\bo, \bs)]$ and substituting the factorized forms:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}
&= \E_{\bq}\!\left[\sum_\sigma \ln \bq_\sigma(s_\sigma) - \sum_\sigma \ln \bp(o_\sigma | s_\sigma) - \sum_\sigma \ln \bp_{\mathcal{M}}(s_\sigma)\right] \nonumber\\
&= \sum_{\sigma \in \mathcal{S}} \E_{\bq_\sigma}\!\left[\ln \bq_\sigma(s_\sigma) - \ln \bp(o_\sigma, s_\sigma)\right]
\label{eq:fmaterial}
\end{align}
where $\bp(o_\sigma, s_\sigma) = \bp(o_\sigma | s_\sigma)\bp_{\mathcal{M}}(s_\sigma)$. Each summand is exactly the semiotic free energy of Sign $\sigma$ (Equation~\ref{eq:semiotic_fe}), giving:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:complexity}
\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}} = \sum_{\sigma \in \mathcal{S}} \mathcal{F}_\sigma
\end{equation}

This result is not a novel quantity but the standard VFE at the fabric level of the nested Markov blanket hierarchy, decomposed via the mean-field approximation. The three assumptions above are idealizations. In practice, domain models introduce correlations between signs through shared ontological structure, and Associations between Signs create observation dependencies that violate the factorized likelihood assumption. The departure from full factorization contributes coupling terms that are neglected under this approximation. The decomposition is therefore exact only in the fully factorized regime; it should be understood as a useful tractable bound rather than an identity.

\label{prop:monotone}
Under typical operating conditions, the dynamics of Process Waves tend to drive this quantity downward over time. Each Wave processing epoch may refine existing Signs by improving their Concept assignments, introduce new Signs that better account for observed data, or assert relations that increase coherence across the fabric. These operations reduce either the semiotic free energy term or the complexity term. Provided that domain models remain stable and data integrity is preserved, the resulting trajectory of $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ is expected to be non-increasing across processing epochs:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:monotone_decrease}
\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}^{(t+1)} \leq \mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}^{(t)}
\end{equation}
Fingerprinting mechanisms prevent redundant re-processing, reducing the risk of oscillatory updates.

From an operational perspective, $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ provides a scalar measure of the fabric’s ``cognitive health''. Decreases indicate that representations are becoming more accurate and coherent, while plateaus suggest saturation. Conversely, increases may signal data corruption, adversarial inputs, or inconsistencies in domain models. Monitoring this quantity in real time would therefore provide a principled, system-level indicator of the fabric’s sense-making capacity.

\section{Computational Demonstration}
\label{sec:simulation}

To validate the formal mapping developed in Sections~\ref{sec:formal}--\ref{sec:fmaterial}, we implement a computational demonstration using discrete active inference following the generative model specification of \texttt{pymdp} \citep{heins2022pymdp}. Rather than simulating Waves on an abstract grid, we ground each experiment in a concrete SemApp operation: Artifact Standard Detection (format-level), Sign disambiguation (content-level), cross-domain entity resolution (relationship-level), and multi-scale free energy minimization (fabric-level). Each experiment implements a Wave as a discrete active inference agent with Bayesian state inference via variational mean-field coordinate ascent, Expected Free Energy (EFE) policy evaluation with pragmatic and epistemic terms, and softmax action selection. The complete simulation code is available in the supplementary materials.

\subsection{Simulation Design Overview}

The four experiments form a progression from the simplest SemApp operation to the full cognitive substrate claim:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{Artifact Standard Detection}: A single Wave infers file formats from noisy cues --- directly modelling SemApp's ASD Wave.
\item \textbf{Ambiguity Resolution}: A single Wave resolves polysemous Signs by progressively accumulating contextual evidence from Associations.
\item \textbf{Multi-Wave Entity Resolution}: Three domain-specialized Waves cooperate through stigmergic coupling to resolve entities across GEOINT, SIGINT, and HUMINT domains.
\item \textbf{Nested Free Energy}: Experiments 1--3 run on a single integrated fabric, tracking uncertainty at element, cluster, domain, and fabric levels.
\end{enumerate}

Each Wave instantiates a factorized generative model $\bp(\bo, \bs) = \prod_m \mathbf{A}_m(\bo_m | \bs) \prod_f \mathbf{D}_f(\bs_f)$ with action-conditioned transitions $\mathbf{B}_f(\bs_f' | \bs_f, \ba)$. State inference uses mean-field variational coordinate ascent:
\begin{equation}
\bq_f(\bs_f) \propto \exp\!\left\{ \E_{\bq_{\setminus f}}\!\left[\ln \bp(\bo, \bs)\right] \right\}
\end{equation}
Policy evaluation minimizes EFE:
\begin{equation}
G(\ba) = \underbrace{-\E_{\bq_{\ba}}\!\left[\ln \bp(\bo)\right]}_{\text{Pragmatic}} + \underbrace{\E_{\bq_{\ba}}\!\left[\mathcal{H}\!\left[\bp(\bo|\bs)\right]\right]}_{\text{Epistemic}}
\end{equation}
with action selection $\pi(\ba) = \sigma(-\gamma \cdot G(\ba))$, where $\gamma$ is the policy precision.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Artifact Standard Detection}
\label{sec:exp1}

\paragraph{Claim.} Even the simplest SemApp operation---classifying an incoming Artifact's file standard---benefits from active inference.

\paragraph{Setup.} A single Wave receives Artifacts and must infer the file standard from noisy, potentially conflicting cues. The generative model has two hidden state factors: \texttt{parse\_status} (2 states: NOT\_PARSED, PARSED) controlled by the agent's action, and \texttt{standard} (6 states: NITF, JPEG2000, GeoTIFF, PDF, XML, UNKNOWN). Four observation modalities provide evidence: magic number match (7 outcomes), file extension (8 outcomes), parse attempt result (7 outcomes: one standard-specific parser match per standard, plus fail and null), and file size range (5 outcomes). The two actions are \textsc{classify} and \textsc{request\_parse}. The key design is that parse\_status is the only action-controlled factor: \textsc{request\_parse} transitions it to PARSED, unlocking standard-specific parser match observations that are unavailable in the NOT\_PARSED state (where only null is observed). This models SemApp's real-world trade-off between cheap heuristic cues and expensive deep parsing.

We generate $N = 500$ artifacts with empirical priors over standards (NITF and GeoTIFF are most common in IC contexts), of which $\sim 30\%$ are \emph{ambiguous}: conflicting magic bytes and file extensions (e.g., GeoTIFF vs.\ plain TIFF sharing magic bytes, or files with wrong extensions). The active inference agent is compared against two baselines operating under the same information constraints: (1)~a \textbf{rule-based priority chain} (magic $\to$ extension $\to$ default), modelling SemApp's current ASD approach, and (2)~a \textbf{maximum-likelihood} classifier using the same likelihood matrices but without EFE-based action selection.

\paragraph{Results.} Figure~\ref{fig:exp1} shows that the active inference Wave achieves $87.8\%$ overall accuracy, outperforming both the rule-based ($80.4\%$) and ML ($79.0\%$) baselines. The advantage is concentrated on ambiguous artifacts ($68.3\%$ vs.\ $51.0\%$ and $48.3\%$), where the agent's EFE-driven decision to request parse operations provides standard-specific parser match evidence that resolves conflicts between noisy magic bytes and file extensions. On clear cases, all methods perform well ($\sim 95\%$). The active inference agent requests parsing on $84\%$ of artifacts, learning when cheap cues suffice and when expensive parsing is epistemically valuable. The confidence calibration plot (panel~c) shows that the active inference agent's posterior probabilities are better calibrated than the baselines, and the VFE distribution (panel~d) shows that incorrect classifications produce systematically higher free energy, confirming VFE as a reliable confidence signal.

\subsection{Experiment 2: Ambiguity Resolution}
\label{sec:exp2}

\paragraph{Claim.} The SRF's relational structure enables active inference to perform genuine semiosis---resolving ambiguity through progressive context accumulation.

\paragraph{Setup.} A single Wave resolves polysemous Signs (e.g., ``Mercury''---planet, element, deity, or car brand) by accumulating contextual evidence from Associations. The generative model has two hidden state factors: \texttt{true\_concept} (5 states: PLANET, ELEMENT, DEITY, CAR\_BRAND, OTHER) and \texttt{context\_domain} (4 states: ASTRONOMY, CHEMISTRY, MYTHOLOGY, AUTOMOTIVE). Three observation modalities provide evidence: \emph{signifier features} (6 outcomes, highly ambiguous for polysemous terms), \emph{association context} (6 outcomes, becoming more informative as more Associations are examined), and \emph{domain model cue} (5 outcomes). The two actions are \textsc{observe\_more} (examine another Association) and \textsc{commit} (assign a Concept).

The association observation model's informativeness increases with each \textsc{observe\_more} action: at step~0, the clarity parameter is $0.05$; by step~5, it reaches $0.90$. This models the SemApp principle that relational context (Associations between Signs) provides richer evidence than the raw Signifier alone. We generate $N = 50$ Signs, of which 15 are genuinely polysemous (drawn from terms like ``Mercury,'' ``Mars,'' ``Saturn'' that appear in multiple concept-domain pairings). Baselines are a \textbf{naive ML assigner} (using signifier features alone, no context) and a \textbf{rule-based context matcher} (using domain cue with hardcoded concept mappings).

\paragraph{Results.} Figure~\ref{fig:exp2} shows that context accumulation dramatically improves accuracy on polysemous Signs: the active inference agent achieves $88.0\%$ overall accuracy ($100\%$ on polysemous cases, $82.9\%$ on unambiguous), vastly outperforming both the naive ML baseline ($48.0\%$, using signifier features alone) and the rule-based context matcher ($58.0\%$). The key mechanism is that at each successive step, only the \emph{new} association context updates the agent's beliefs---signifier and domain cues, already incorporated in the empirical prior, are masked with uniform likelihoods to prevent evidence re-counting. The agent reaches confident ($\geq 70\%$ posterior) commitment after an average of only 3.9 inference steps (out of a maximum of 7), demonstrating that progressive Bayesian accumulation efficiently identifies when enough context has been gathered. The VFE trace (panel~c) shows that polysemous Signs start with higher free energy and converge more slowly, reflecting the greater inferential challenge they pose.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Multi-Wave Entity Resolution}
\label{sec:exp3}

\paragraph{Claim.} The PWF's shared fabric enables cooperative intelligence through overlapping Markov blankets.

\paragraph{Setup.} Three domain-specialized Waves (GEOINT, SIGINT, HUMINT) cooperate through a shared fabric of 30 Signs (10 per domain) to resolve references to 8 underlying entities. Five of these entities span multiple domains (cross-domain entities), creating the need for cross-domain inference. Each Wave has a factorized generative model with two hidden state factors (\texttt{entity\_id} with 8 states, \texttt{domain\_source} with 3 states) and four observation modalities (feature pattern, domain signature, cross-domain link, temporal correlation). The three actions are \textsc{observe}, \textsc{link\_same}, and \textsc{link\_different}.

Domain-specialized Waves have enhanced perceptual precision ($\kappa = 0.70$) for entities in their own domain, domain-specific entity priors (concentrated on entities known to appear in their domain), and preferences that favour their domain's observations. \emph{Stigmergic coupling} is the key mechanism: when Wave~A asserts an entity link, this becomes visible to Waves~B and~C as a cross-domain link observation, modelling the shared Markov blanket through which Waves communicate indirectly by modifying the fabric.

We compare four conditions: (1)~\textbf{cooperative specialized}: 3 domain-expert Waves with stigmergic coupling; (2)~\textbf{single generalist}: 1 Wave with no domain expertise processing all domains; (3)~\textbf{isolated specialized}: 3 domain-expert Waves without coupling; and (4)~\textbf{homogeneous cooperative}: 3 identical non-expert Waves with coupling.

\paragraph{Results.} Averaged over 20 replications to control for stochastic variation, Figure~\ref{fig:exp3} shows the predicted ordering: cooperative specialized Waves achieve $94.0\% \pm 0.6\%$ (SE) accuracy, significantly outperforming isolated specialized ($85.3\% \pm 1.1\%$), homogeneous cooperative ($61.2\% \pm 1.4\%$), and single generalist ($56.2\% \pm 1.4\%$). The cooperative advantage arises from \emph{belief propagation through links}: when two cross-domain Signs are confidently linked (both posterior maxima exceeding $0.60$ and agreeing on entity), the linked Sign's entity posterior is blended into the focal Sign's empirical prior, carrying entity-specific information that disambiguates within-domain feature collisions. The entity patterns are designed so that cross-domain entities share feature patterns with single-domain entities (e.g., entities 0 and 5 share pattern~0 in GEOINT), creating confusion pairs that can only be resolved through cross-domain evidence. The homogeneous cooperative condition performs worse than isolated despite coupling, demonstrating that stigmergic coordination amplifies the effect of generative model quality---poor models propagate noise through the shared fabric. The entity confusion matrix (panel~e) shows that cooperative specialized Waves are most confused between entities with similar feature patterns, consistent with the Bayesian structure of the inference.

\subsection{Experiment 4: Nested Free Energy Minimization}
\label{sec:exp4}

\paragraph{Claim.} The fabric exhibits multi-scale self-organization---the signature of a cognitive system.

\paragraph{Setup.} We run Experiments 1--3 sequentially on a single integrated fabric of 100 elements (20 Artifacts from Experiment~1, 50 Signs from Experiment~2, and 30 Signs from Experiment~3), tracking posterior entropy $H[\bq(\bs)]$ as a proxy for variational free energy at four scales: individual elements, entity/concept clusters, processing domains, and the full fabric. The key theoretical prediction is that fabric-level uncertainty should decrease monotonically even when individual elements show discrete jumps, because higher-level Markov blankets integrate and smooth lower-level dynamics.

\paragraph{Results.} Figure~\ref{fig:exp4} confirms the multi-scale prediction. Fabric-level uncertainty decreases monotonically from $1.79$ to $0.29$ nats across the three processing phases. Individual elements (panel~a) show characteristic discrete jumps as they are processed---Artifacts drop sharply from $\log 6 \approx 1.79$ nats upon classification, Signs decrease progressively during disambiguation, and entity Signs are updated repeatedly during multi-Wave processing. At the cluster and domain levels (panel~b), the uncertainty curves are smoother, integrating the effects of multiple element-level transitions. The multi-scale comparison (panel~c) shows progressively smoother curves at higher aggregation levels, exactly as predicted by the nested Markov blanket structure: the fabric-level free energy is the mean-field average of element-level free energies, and the law of large numbers ensures that higher-level quantities are more regular than their lower-level components.

\subsection{Results Synthesis}

The four experiments form a coherent argument for the cognitive substrate thesis:

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
\item \textbf{Format-level} (Experiment~1): Active inference outperforms rigid heuristics on ambiguous Artifacts, validating the Wave-as-agent formalization for the simplest SemApp operation.
\item \textbf{Content-level} (Experiment~2): Progressive context accumulation through Associations enables genuine semiosis---resolving polysemous Signs through the same abductive inference process formalized in Section~\ref{sec:signs_vfe}.
\item \textbf{Relationship-level} (Experiment~3): Domain-specialized Waves cooperating through belief propagation via stigmergic links outperform isolated, generalist, and homogeneous configurations ($94.0\%$ vs.\ $85.3\%$, $56.2\%$, $61.2\%$), validating the overlapping Markov blanket model of Section~\ref{sec:network}.
\item \textbf{Fabric-level} (Experiment~4): The integrated fabric exhibits multi-scale self-organization, with monotonically decreasing fabric-level free energy emerging from discrete element-level dynamics, validating the nested blanket structure of Section~\ref{sec:nested}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:implications}

SemApp provides what may be the first operational, production-scale instantiation of nested Markov blankets in a computational system. While the theory of nested blankets has been developed extensively in the context of biological systems \citep{friston2019particular, kirchhoff2018markov, ramstead2023inner}, its application to engineered data systems is novel. The SemApp deployment at NGA, processing petabytes of data with hundreds of concurrent users over nearly a decade, constitutes an empirical test bed for nested blanket theory at a scale unprecedented in the literature.

The Peirce--FEP isomorphism suggests that semiotics is a \emph{consequence} of the FEP applied to sign systems. If this isomorphism holds as strongly as the formal analysis suggests, then any system performing active inference over meaningful content is, by definition, performing semiosis. This has implications for the biosemiotics literature, which has independently argued that life is a semiotic process.

The multi-Wave dynamics of the PWF provide a test bed for the epistemic community theory of \citet{albarracin2022epistemic}. Experiment~3 demonstrates that Wave ecosystems exhibit the same dynamics as social epistemic communities: domain-specialized Waves with distinct generative models ($\mathbf{A}$, $\mathbf{C}$, $\mathbf{D}$) contribute complementary expertise to collective sense-making, while homogeneous configurations lacking epistemic diversity perform worst despite having access to the same stigmergic coupling mechanisms. Each generative model component contributes a distinct mechanism---perceptual precision ($\mathbf{A}$) determines domain expertise, preferences ($\mathbf{C}$) guide action selection toward informative observations, and priors ($\mathbf{D}$) encode domain-specific expectations---while misspecified models (the homogeneous condition) propagate noise through the shared fabric. This opens the possibility of engineering Wave ecosystems with designed epistemic properties.


\subsection{For DoD/IC Data Architecture}

Active Inference has useful predictive design principles for next-generation data architectures that can be articulated without reducing them to discrete design heuristics. First, the framework implies the importance of epistemic diversity. Heterogeneous ecosystems of Waves consistently outperform homogeneous configurations. This suggests that data architectures should be structured to encourage third-party Wave development and pluralistic modelling approaches, rather than concentrating processing within a small number of centralized pipelines.

Second, classification emerges as a structural requirement for maintaining Markov blanket integrity. Classification boundaries sustain the conditional independence assumptions that allow modular inference to proceed. Weakening these controls would therefore erode the very separability that enables scalable, distributed sense-making.

Third, provenance should be understood as a form of cognitive memory within the system. Fingerprints and Perceiver metadata instantiate the temporal depth required for non-Markovian inference. Systems that discard provenance effectively eliminate their own capacity for historical integration and thus become functionally amnesic.

Fourth, the fabric-level variational free energy $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ is a useful scalar quantity for operational monitoring. Because it captures the alignment between observations, beliefs, and inferred structure, it can serve as a global health metric for the fabric. In practice, a real-time dashboard tracking $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ would offer operators direct visibility into the system's ongoing sense-making capacity.

Domain models in Layer 3 should be reconceptualised as adaptive priors rather than fixed schemas. Treating them as updateable belief structures allows the system to refine its inductive biases through experience, aligning with SemApp’s design philosophy of optional, rather than mandatory, standardisation.

\subsection{Designing Explainable Semiotic AI}

\citet{albarracin2023explainable} argue that active inference, with its explicit generative models, is inherently explainable. Every action can be traced to a belief, every belief to an observation, and every observation to a specific element of the generative model. Applied to SemApp, this implies that every Wave enrichment can be rendered intelligible through its DEEP decomposition. An enrichment may be attributed to deontic factors when it arises because the classification policy mandates it, reflecting the normative constraints imposed on the system. It may instead be epistemic in nature when the enrichment serves to reduce uncertainty about the referent of a Sign, thereby improving the system’s inferential accuracy. In other cases, the enrichment is pragmatic, insofar as it contributes to bringing the fabric into closer alignment with the domain model, effectively guiding action toward goal-consistent states. Finally, an enrichment can be preferential when it reflects the expectations encoded in the domain model, such that a particular Concept is predicted for a given Signifier.

When this decomposition is combined with fingerprint-based provenance and the complete enrichment history of the fabric, explainability emerges as an intrinsic property of the system. Rather than being retrofitted as a post-hoc interpretability layer, it is built directly into the architecture of active inference, ensuring that each transformation of the data can be traced, justified, and understood in terms of its underlying generative model.

\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Limitations}

The formal mapping presented in this paper is \emph{structural}. We show that SemApp's architecture has the form of a nested Markov blanket system performing active inference. We do not claim that SemApp was designed with the FEP in mind (it was not), nor that its developers intended to implement active inference. Rather, we argue that the architecture converged on an FEP-consistent design because the FEP describes the necessary conditions for any system that maintains its organization while making sense of a complex environment. This convergent design is, if anything, stronger evidence for the framework's explanatory power than a design that deliberately instantiated FEP principles.

The simulation is a proof of concept, not a model of SemApp at operational scale. Experiment~1's six file standards, Experiment~2's $5 \times 4 = 20$ joint states, and Experiment~3's $8 \times 3 = 24$ joint states are many orders of magnitude smaller than SemApp's actual state space ($\sim 70$ PB of data across millions of Signs). Scaling to realistic dimensions would require approximate inference methods (e.g., amortized variational inference, message passing on factor graphs) beyond the scope of this paper. Importantly, Experiment~1 directly models a real SemApp Wave---the Artifact Standard Detection pipeline---and its observation modalities (magic bytes, file extensions, parse results, file size) correspond to actual cues used in production. Experiments~2--4 are more stylized but remain grounded in real SemApp data types: Signs, Associations, Concepts, and domain models are all first-class entities in the SemApp architecture, and the polysemous terms, cross-domain entities, and stigmergic coupling mechanisms modelled in the simulation reflect genuine challenges faced by operational fabrics.

The conditional independence claim holds at the architectural level (by design, Artifacts and domain models interact only through Signs), but could be violated in practice by side channels (e.g., file naming conventions that leak domain model information into Layer 1). A formal verification of blanket integrity in the running system is an open challenge.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Several lines of future work follow naturally from this analysis and point toward both theoretical and practical extensions of the framework. A first direction concerns the online monitoring of fabric-level free energy, $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$. Implementing real-time computation of this quantity would yield a principled operational metric for assessing the fabric's sense-making capacity as it evolves. Our simulation (Experiment~4) demonstrates that $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ is computable in a tractable discrete setting and that it decreases monotonically across processing phases; scaling to production settings necessitates the development of efficient approximation schemes that preserve interpretability while remaining computationally feasible.

A second direction reframes Wave design as an instance of generative model engineering. If Waves are properly understood as active inference agents, then specifying a Wave amounts to defining its underlying generative model. Within this perspective, the DEEP decomposition is a structured design language. Epistemic value determines what the Wave should seek in terms of uncertainty reduction, pragmatic value specifies what outcomes it should realise, and deontic value encodes the constraints under which it must operate. This reframing opens the door to systematic methodologies for constructing, comparing, and validating Waves.

A third avenue concerns multi-fabric federation. SemApp already enables distributed querying across instances, but under the present framework this capability can be interpreted more deeply as distributed active inference across organizational Markov blankets. We could thus analyse inter-agency information sharing, where each fabric maintains partial autonomy while participating in a larger inferential ecosystem.

A fourth line of work addresses adversarial robustness, which can be recast as the problem of defending Markov blanket integrity. From this perspective, data poisoning and adversarial injection are not merely anomalies but targeted disruptions of the conditional independencies that sustain coherent inference. The Free Energy Principle has a principled basis for detection and mitigation. Systematic increases in $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ can be interpreted as signals of blanket violation, thereby enabling early identification of compromised states.

Finally, these directions connect directly to the broader vision of ecosystems of intelligence articulated by \citet{friston2022ecosystems}, in which natural and artificial agents are coupled through shared generative models. SemApp’s fabric, with its unified API and Wave abstraction, provides a concrete substrate for realising such ecosystems, enabling coordinated sense-making across heterogeneous agents while preserving modularity and local autonomy.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have argued that the SemApp semiotic data fabric is not merely a data platform but a cognitive substrate, a system that satisfies the formal criteria for cognition under the Free Energy Principle. The argument rests on three pillars:

First, \textbf{semiosis is inference}. The Peirce--FEP correspondence (Section~\ref{sec:signs_vfe}) establishes that Sign creation in SemApp, binding Signifiers with Concepts, is variational inference that minimizes semiotic free energy. The fabric does not merely store data; it \emph{interprets} it, generating meaning through the same formal process that defines cognition under the FEP.

Second, \textbf{the architecture is a nested Markov blanket system} (Section~\ref{sec:nested}). The three-layer architecture, with Artifacts as external states, Signs as blanket states, and domain models as internal states, satisfies the conditional independence requirements of the blanket partition. Each Sign has its own blanket nested within the layer blanket, nested within the fabric blanket, the recursive structure that \citet{kirchhoff2018markov} identify as the hallmark of cognitive systems.

Third, \textbf{Process Waves are active inference agents} that coordinate through shared Markov blankets (Section~\ref{sec:network}), creating the network effect that makes the fabric progressively more capable. The fabric-level variational free energy $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ (Section~\ref{sec:fmaterial}), derived from the fabric's generative model under a mean-field factorization, provides a principled metric for this progressive sense-making, and its tendency to decrease under consistent processing, validated by simulation, constitutes the fabric's learning.

The computational demonstration validates this framework through four experiments of increasing scope: a single Wave outperforms rigid heuristics on ambiguous Artifact classification (Experiment~1), progressive context accumulation enables genuine semiosis---achieving $100\%$ accuracy on polysemous Signs versus $48\%$ for static maximum-likelihood (Experiment~2), domain-specialized Waves cooperating through belief propagation via stigmergic links outperform isolated, generalist, and homogeneous configurations at $94.0\%$ vs.\ $85.3\%$, $56.2\%$, and $61.2\%$ respectively (Experiment~3), and an integrated fabric of 100 elements exhibits monotonically decreasing free energy from $1.79$ to $0.29$ nats at the fabric level despite discrete jumps at the element level (Experiment~4)---the multi-scale self-organization that is the signature of a cognitive system.

Nearly a decade of operational experience at NGA, processing tens of petabytes of data and supporting mission-critical intelligence operations, provides empirical validation at a scale unprecedented for nested Markov blanket theory. The framework not only explains why SemApp works but predicts how to extend it: through epistemic Wave diversity, adaptive domain models, and $\mathcal{F}_{\text{fabric}}$ monitoring.

We believe this analysis opens a door to a new class of data architectures, \emph{cognitive data fabrics}, that are designed from first principles to make sense of the world. In the spirit of \citet{friston2022ecosystems}, such fabrics may form the substrate for ecosystems of intelligence in which humans, algorithms, and institutions coordinate through shared semiotic structures, achieving collective sense-making at the scale that the challenges of the 21st century demand.
